We build the website instead of pitching it

        field notes
        
Most studios send a deck. We sent you a universe that assembles a working app, writes a website from code, reads a review and answers it, and brands a company in front of you — live, in your browser.

        
It's deliberate. Anyone can claim they're good; proof is harder, and it's the only thing worth your time. If a self-running demonstration of the work doesn't convince you, a slide certainly won't.

        
So treat this whole site as the portfolio. Everything you scrolled through was made the way we make things for clients: from a blank file, by people who've shipped a lot of them.

      

      

        
Price disruption is an engineering problem

        on pricing
        
Cheap usually means worse. We think that's a failure of process, not a law of nature. The agency price tag is mostly overhead, account managers and junior rework — not the actual building.

        
Strip those away. Put senior people on the tools, lean on modern frameworks and AI for the repetitive 80%, and reuse hard-won patterns instead of reinventing them. What's left is the part that creates value — and it costs a fraction of what you were quoted.

        
That's the company in one line: the quality of the expensive shops, at a price a small business can say yes to — not by cutting corners, but by removing the parts that were never worth paying for.

      

      

        
What a small business should build first

        for owners
        
Not everything at once. The fastest path is usually one sharp thing that removes a daily headache or captures a sale you're currently losing — a booking flow, an order page, an automation that answers the same question for the hundredth time.

        
Ship that, let it earn, then compound. A focused first build pays for the next one and shows you exactly where the demand is. Big-bang launches mostly burn budget proving things you could have learned in two weeks.

        
When we scope a project, the first question isn't "what could we build" — it's "what's the smallest thing that's clearly worth it." Then we make that excellent.

      

      

        
Two weeks to something you can hold

        how we work
        
Shape (wk 1–2) — a clickable prototype you can react to. Real screens, real flow, no lorem ipsum. You'll know if it's right before a line of production code exists.

        
Build (wk 3–6) — the working product, with real data and real users. Senior hands, tight loops, nothing parked in a queue.

        
Launch (wk 7+) — ship it, measure it, make it spread, then keep improving what the numbers point to.

        
The first demo lands in your hands about two weeks after kickoff. Fast isn't the opposite of good here — it's how we keep the price honest.
